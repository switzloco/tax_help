# Agent Skill: Hobby Loss Rules (IRC Section 183 & TCJA)

This skill defines the rules for distinguishing between a bona fide business activity (conducted for profit) and a hobby (not engaged in for profit), under current IRC Section 183 and Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) regulations.

## 1. The Profit Presumption Test (IRC § 183(d))

An activity is presumed to be carried on for profit if it produces a profit in at least **3 out of 5 consecutive years** ending with the tax year in question (or 2 out of 7 years for activities involving horses).
- If the business fails this test (e.g., claiming losses year after year), the burden of proof shifts to the taxpayer to demonstrate a genuine profit motive.
- In determining profit motive, the IRS evaluates nine non-exclusive factors (Treas. Reg. § 1.183-2(b)):
  1. Manner in which the taxpayer carries on the activity (business-like records, separate bank accounts).
  2. The expertise of the taxpayer or their advisors.
  3. The time and effort expended by the taxpayer.
  4. Expectation that assets used in the activity may appreciate in value.
  5. The success of the taxpayer in carrying on other similar or dissimilar activities.
  6. The taxpayer's history of income or losses with respect to the activity.
  7. The amount of occasional profits, if any, which are earned.
  8. The financial status of the taxpayer (e.g., whether W-2 income subsidizes losses).
  9. Elements of personal pleasure or recreation.

## 2. Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) Deductibility Rules

Under the TCJA (tax years 2018–2025 and ongoing under current extensions):
- **Income Reporting:** ALL gross receipts from a hobby MUST be reported on the tax return.
  - Reported on: **Form 1040, Schedule 1, Part I, Line 8z ("Other Income")**.
  - This income is subject to federal income tax and flows directly into Adjusted Gross Income (AGI).
- **Expense Deductions:** **ZERO expense deductions are allowed.**
  - Previously, hobby expenses could be deducted on Schedule A as miscellaneous itemized deductions subject to the 2% AGI floor.
  - Under IRC § 67(g) (added by TCJA), all miscellaneous itemized deductions subject to the 2% floor are suspended.
  - Consequently, taxpayers cannot deduct any expenses associated with a hobby, resulting in 100% taxability of gross hobby receipts.

> [!WARNING]
> If a side business is classified as a hobby due to failing the 3-out-of-5-year profit test and lacking profit motive:
> - Do NOT report income and expenses on Schedule C.
> - Report gross revenue on Schedule 1 as Other Income.
> - Disallow all business expenses, depreciation, and home office deductions.
> - The Audit Agent must flag any Schedule C losses that appear to violate IRC § 183.

## 3. Tax Planning: Converting a Hobby to a Business (Claiming Schedule C Losses)

To defend deductions and claim a net loss on Schedule C that offsets high W-2 income, the taxpayer must actively restructure the activity to prove a profit motive. The Audit Agent should recommend the following concrete steps:

1. **Maintain Separate Books & Accounts:** Immediately open a dedicated business checking account and credit card. Keep clean digital records (e.g. QuickBooks). Never commingle personal and business funds (Treas. Reg. § 1.183-2(b)(1)).
2. **Form a Legal Entity (LLC):** Register the business as a single-member LLC with the state and obtain an EIN. This signals business-like intent.
3. **Draft a Written Business Plan:** Create a formal business plan showing:
   - A description of the market and target customer base.
   - A clear profit forecast.
   - Concrete steps taken or planned to turn the loss into a profit (e.g., changing advertising methods, adjusting pricing, dropping unprofitable products).
4. **Acquire Professional Advice:** Consult a CPA or business consultant and document their recommendations. Implementing expert feedback is a primary factor proving profit intent (Treas. Reg. § 1.183-2(b)(2)).
5. **Dedicate Substantial Time:** Maintain a log of hours spent on the business, showing that the time spent is substantial and focused on commercial viability rather than pure recreation (Treas. Reg. § 1.183-2(b)(3)).
